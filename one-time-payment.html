Collections are offered as standalone one-time acquisitions, with no ongoing access fees to the collections purchased.

Each Wiley Digital Archives collection is offered as a one-time purchase, with no on-going hosting or access fees. Each acquisition becomes part of your institution’s permanent holdings complete with MARC records and KBART files.

Wiley Digital Archives collections can be acquired separately or in bundles. When multiple archives are purchased, users can utilize the cross-search capability to engage with a broad range of primary source materials, or search within an individual society archive for a more targeted research experience. The pricing for each Wiley Digital Archives collection is derived from several factors. These considerations include, but are not limited to, the research value of the content, uniqueness or rarity of the primary source materials, the amount of cataloging and conservation required to digitize the collection, size and amount of content within, and other factors such as the full-time enrollment (FTE) at a given institution.

Training for librarians, faculty, researchers, and students is free, and is gladly offered from Wiley.

Each Wiley Digital Archives collection is inherently multi-disciplinary and can provide a wealth of content to a wide range of academic needs, including educational uses within a classroom setting or through course management systems. Because they are multifaceted, each of the archive collections has the capacity to serve a broad number of departmental needs and research interests across campus and can be integrated into LibGuides and curricula.

Imagine if your institution had to provide multiple travel or research grants to successive researchers and students across departments and programs to visit one physical archive? These one-time investments allow for a comparatively small number of scholars to gain a period of access to these analog content repositories, and traditional access still does not solve for the discovery needs—many physical archive visits involve more time spent searching for content and less time thinking critically about the content itself.
